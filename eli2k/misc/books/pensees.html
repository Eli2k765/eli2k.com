Pensées ~ 1670

Last updated: May 16, 2025

Introduction and Historical Context

Blaise Pascal (1623-1662) was a French mathematician, physicist, inventor, philosopher, and Catholic theologian whose unfinished collection of thoughts and notes, posthumously titled Pensées (French for "Thoughts"), stands as one of the most profound religious and philosophical works of Western literature. Published eight years after his death, this fragmentary masterpiece was originally intended as a comprehensive defense of Christianity, an apologetic work that was never completed due to Pascal's untimely death at age 39.

To understand the Pensées, one must grasp the intellectual climate of 17th-century France. This was the age of the Scientific Revolution, when thinkers like Descartes were challenging traditional ways of understanding the world. It was also a time of religious conflict following the Protestant Reformation, with intense debates between Jesuits and Jansenists (a Catholic movement emphasizing original sin and divine grace that influenced Pascal). The emerging philosophical skepticism of figures like Montaigne posed serious challenges to religious certainty, while libertinism was becoming fashionable among the aristocracy.

Pascal himself underwent a profound religious experience on November 23, 1654 – his "night of fire" – which he recorded on a piece of parchment (known as the "Memorial") that he kept sewn into his coat lining until his death. This mystical encounter transformed him from a brilliant scientist into a devoted religious thinker, though he never abandoned his mathematical rigor and psychological insight.

Structure and Editorial History

The Pensées are not a completed work but rather a collection of notes, fragments, and partially developed arguments that Pascal jotted down for his planned defense of Christianity. After his death, his family and the Port-Royal community (associated with Jansenism) published an edited version in 1670, arranging the fragments in what they considered a logical order. Later editions, particularly the critical edition by Louis Lafuma in the 20th century, attempted to restore the fragments to their original order based on Pascal's own classification system.

The fragments vary greatly in length and development – some are mere phrases or sentences, while others extend to several pages of careful argument. This fragmentary quality gives the work a strikingly modern feel, as if we are witnessing Pascal's mind at work, thinking through difficult problems without always reaching tidy conclusions.

Modern readers should understand that what we call the Pensées is essentially a work in progress, a glimpse into the workshop of one of history's greatest minds. The unfinished nature of the text has allowed for diverse interpretations and continues to give the work a sense of immediacy and openness.

Major Philosophical Themes

The Pensées cover an extraordinary range of topics, but several major themes emerge that form the backbone of Pascal's religious and philosophical thinking:

Human Contradiction: Perhaps the most famous aspect of Pascal's thought is his exploration of the contradictory nature of human existence. He writes, "What a chimera, then, is man! What a novelty, what a monster, what a chaos, what a subject of contradiction, what a prodigy! Judge of all things, feeble earthworm, repository of truth, sewer of uncertainty and error, the glory and the refuse of the universe!" For Pascal, humans are simultaneously great and wretched – great in our capacity for thought and self-awareness, wretched in our mortality and moral failings.

The Three Orders: Pascal distinguishes between three orders or realms of reality: the order of the body/flesh (physical power), the order of mind (intellectual ability), and the order of charity/heart (moral and spiritual insight). Each has its own kind of greatness that is incommensurable with the others – a king may have physical power, a scientist intellectual achievement, but neither necessarily possesses spiritual wisdom.

Reason and Faith: Unlike many religious thinkers, Pascal doesn't see faith as opposed to reason but rather as extending beyond reason's limits. "The heart has its reasons which reason knows nothing of," he famously writes. For Pascal, there are truths that can only be grasped through the "heart" (not mere emotion but a deeper mode of understanding). Religious faith isn't irrational but transrational – it begins where reason reaches its limits.

Diversion (Divertissement): Pascal offers a penetrating analysis of how humans use entertainment, business, and various activities to distract themselves from contemplating their own mortality and the fundamental questions of existence. "All of humanity's problems stem from man's inability to sit quietly in a room alone," he observes. Our constant need for diversion reveals our discomfort with our true condition.

Pascal's Wager

The most famous argument in the Pensées is undoubtedly "Pascal's Wager" (though Pascal himself never called it that). This pioneering application of probability theory to religious belief suggests that even if God's existence cannot be proven through reason alone, belief is still the rational choice.

The argument runs roughly as follows: If God exists and you believe, you gain infinite reward (eternal happiness); if God exists and you don't believe, you risk infinite loss (damnation). If God doesn't exist and you believe, you lose little (some pleasures, perhaps); if God doesn't exist and you don't believe, you gain little. Given these stakes, the rational choice is to "wager" on God's existence.

This argument has been widely misinterpreted as a cynical calculation, but Pascal's point is more subtle. He isn't suggesting people fake belief for personal gain. Rather, he's addressing those who find themselves unable to decide on God's existence through reason alone. The wager is meant as a starting point – Pascal suggests that by acting as if one believes (attending Mass, etc.), genuine faith may follow. "Kneel down, move your lips in prayer, and you will believe."

Critics have objected that the wager creates a false dichotomy (ignoring other religions) or that belief cannot be chosen. Nevertheless, the argument remains influential in philosophy of religion and decision theory for its innovative use of probabilistic reasoning in matters of faith.

Literary Style and Rhetorical Strategy

For all its philosophical depth, the Pensées is also a literary masterpiece. Pascal's style – concise, vivid, and often aphoristic – represents the pinnacle of French classical prose. He combines mathematical precision with poetic intensity, creating memorable phrases that continue to be quoted centuries later. "The eternal silence of these infinite spaces frightens me" is just one example of his ability to express profound existential insights in haunting language.

As a planned apologetic work, the Pensées reveals Pascal's sophisticated understanding of his audience – the sophisticated, skeptical intellectuals of his day. Rather than beginning with scriptural authority or church doctrine, Pascal starts by analyzing the human condition in ways his secular contemporaries would recognize. Only after establishing the contradictory nature of human existence does he suggest that Christianity uniquely explains this contradiction through the doctrine of the Fall and redemption.

Pascal's approach is psychological as much as logical. He aims not just to convince the intellect but to move the will. Understanding that humans are not purely rational beings, he appeals to emotion, imagination, and self-interest alongside reason. This literary power is why even readers who reject Pascal's religious conclusions remain captivated by his insights into human nature.

The "God-shaped Void" and Human Restlessness

One of Pascal's most influential concepts (though not directly phrased this way in the text) is the idea that humans have a "God-shaped void" in their hearts. "There is a God-shaped vacuum in the heart of each man which cannot be satisfied by any created thing but only by God the Creator, made known through Jesus Christ," is a popular paraphrase of Pascal's thinking.

What Pascal actually writes is: "What else does this craving, and this helplessness, proclaim but that there was once in man a true happiness, of which all that now remains is the empty print and trace? This he tries in vain to fill with everything around him... though none can help, since this infinite abyss can be filled only with an infinite and immutable object; in other words by God himself."

This insight into human restlessness and our endless searching for fulfillment has resonated far beyond Christian circles. Even secular readers recognize Pascal's psychological acuity in identifying the persistent human sense that something fundamental is missing from our lives. Augustine had expressed a similar idea in his Confessions ("Our hearts are restless until they rest in You"), but Pascal develops this theme with particular psychological depth.

For Pascal, our endless pursuit of diversions, achievements, and pleasures demonstrates that we are trying to fill a void that only the infinite can satisfy. Our greatness lies in recognizing this void; our wretchedness in our futile attempts to fill it with finite things.

Philosophy of Knowledge and the "Reasons of the Heart"

Pascal's epistemology (theory of knowledge) represents a middle path between rationalism and fideism. Against pure rationalists like Descartes, Pascal insists that reason has limits: "The last step of reason is to recognize that there are an infinity of things which surpass it." Against pure fideists who reject reason altogether, Pascal maintains that reason plays a crucial role in understanding religious truth.

His famous statement that "The heart has its reasons which reason knows nothing of" introduces what we might call a form of personal or experiential knowledge that complements rational understanding. By "heart" (cœur), Pascal doesn't mean mere emotion but rather an intuitive capacity for grasping first principles and fundamental truths directly.

We know space, time, motion, and number through this immediate intuition, not through rational demonstration. Similarly, we know moral and religious truths through the heart. This doesn't make these truths subjective or arbitrary – for Pascal, the heart is a genuine cognitive faculty, not just a source of feelings.

This aspect of Pascal's thought has proven particularly influential in existentialist philosophy, which similarly emphasizes forms of truth that cannot be reduced to objective, scientific knowledge. It also anticipates modern discussions about the limits of purely analytical approaches to human experience.

The Hidden God (Deus Absconditus)

A central theological concept in the Pensées is that of the "hidden God" (Deus absconditus). Drawing on Isaiah 45:15 ("Truly, you are a God who hides himself"), Pascal explores why God doesn't make his existence obvious to all through undeniable evidence.

For Pascal, God provides enough light for those who desire to see and enough darkness for those who have a contrary disposition. This hiddenness is not arbitrary but purposeful – it respects human freedom and reveals the state of our hearts. Those who sincerely seek God will find sufficient evidence; those who don't wish to find God can always find reasons to doubt.

"What appears in nature as marking the existence of a God are neither utterly clear nor utterly obscure. There is light for those who desire only to see, and darkness for those who have a contrary desire."

This careful balance of revelation and concealment means that religious faith always involves a choice. The evidence doesn't compel belief in a mathematical sense, but it invites it. This theme resonates with contemporary discussions about why God might permit doubt and why religious experience isn't more universally shared.

Cultural Impact and Legacy

The influence of the Pensées extends far beyond theology or philosophy proper. Pascal's penetrating psychological insights, literary style, and existential depth have made the work a touchstone for thinkers of various backgrounds.

In philosophy, Pascal helped pioneer existentialist themes later developed by Kierkegaard, Nietzsche, Heidegger, and Sartre. His emphasis on the concrete human situation, on anxiety in the face of death, and on the limits of abstract reason all anticipate major currents in existentialist thought. Even atheist existentialists like Camus found Pascal's diagnosis of the human condition compelling, if not his religious solution.

In literature, Pascal's influence can be seen in writers from Dostoyevsky to T.S. Eliot, who were drawn to his portrayal of modern alienation and his critique of rationalism without faith. His aphoristic style influenced French moralists like La Rochefoucauld, while his psychological depth inspired the novel of consciousness.

In religious thought, Pascal offers a path between rigid scholasticism and anti-intellectual fideism. His approach to apologetics, beginning with human experience rather than abstract theology, continues to influence how Christianity is presented to skeptics. His integration of emotional, volitional, and intellectual elements in religious conviction speaks to contemporary holistic approaches to spirituality.

Even in science and mathematics, fields Pascal himself contributed to significantly, his recognition of the limits of the scientific method and his insistence that the deepest human questions cannot be resolved by calculation alone remain pertinent critiques of scientism.

Reading the Pensées Today

For the modern reader approaching the Pensées for the first time, a few suggestions might enhance the experience:

First, embrace the fragmentary nature of the text. Don't expect a systematic treatise with clear beginnings and conclusions. The unfinished quality of the work invites us to think alongside Pascal rather than simply receiving his finished thoughts.

Second, consider reading thematically rather than sequentially. The various orderings of the fragments (whether the Port-Royal, Lafuma, or Sellier editions) all impose a structure that wasn't finalized by Pascal himself. Feel free to follow threads of interest through the text.

Third, read both with and against Pascal. While his religious conclusions may not convince everyone, his diagnosis of the human condition transcends theological boundaries. Even staunch atheists have found profound value in his psychological insights.

Finally, consider how Pascal's critiques apply to our contemporary moment. His analysis of diversion seems even more relevant in our age of constant digital distraction. His thoughts on the limits of scientific reasoning speak to current debates about science and meaning. His recognition of the human need for transcendence addresses our persistent spiritual hunger despite increasing secularization.

The Pensées remains vital because it addresses perennial questions about human existence with unusual honesty, depth, and literary power. Whether we share Pascal's Christian faith or not, his unflinching confrontation with mortality, meaning, and the human yearning for transcendence continues to challenge and inspire readers across the centuries.

Personal Reflection

Reading the Pensées is something of a paradox – it's a deeply personal work that somehow speaks directly to each reader's own existential situation. When Pascal writes about our discomfort with solitude or our attempts to distract ourselves from the fundamental questions of existence, many of us recognize ourselves with uncomfortable clarity.

Perhaps you've experienced that moment of unexpected quiet when the diversions fall away, and you're confronted with questions about your own mortality and the meaning of your life. Pascal helps us see that our avoidance of these moments isn't accidental but systematic – and that this very avoidance might be preventing us from discovering deeper truths about ourselves.

What makes the Pensées so enduring is that it doesn't simply present abstract theological arguments but invites us into a kind of spiritual self-examination. Pascal writes as someone who has peered into the abyss of existential anxiety and found, not simple answers, but a faith that embraces the mystery of the human condition.

Whether you approach this text as a believer, a skeptic, or somewhere in between, Pascal offers a rare combination of intellectual rigor and spiritual depth. In an age of superficial solutions and digital distractions, his voice calls us back to the questions that matter most – questions we might prefer to avoid but which define our humanity. The Pensées doesn't allow us the comfort of easy certainties, whether religious or secular, but invites us into a more authentic confrontation with ourselves and with ultimate reality.
