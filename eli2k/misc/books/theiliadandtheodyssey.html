The Iliad and The Odyssey by Homer ~ 800BC

Last updated: March 5th, 2023

Context

The Iliad and The Odyssey are attributed to Homer, a poet who likely lived in the 8th century BCE, though his exact identity remains debated by scholars. These epics weren't merely entertainment but served as foundational cultural texts for ancient Greeks, encoding their values, religious beliefs, and heroic ideals. Both epics are set against the backdrop of the Trojan War, which many historians believe was based on actual conflicts between Mycenaean Greeks and the city of Troy around 1200 BCE. While archaeological evidence confirms Troy's existence (at the site now called Hisarlik in modern Turkey), the specific events of the war as portrayed in the epics blend historical elements with mythological elaboration. The poems were originally composed in dactylic hexameter and transmitted orally through generations of bards before being written down, likely during the 8th century BCE. This oral tradition explains certain features of the poems, including formulaic expressions, repeated epithets, and episodic structure. For ancient Greeks, these works weren't just literature but contained essential cultural knowledge about:


  • The pantheon of gods and their relationships with humans
  • Proper conduct and heroic values
  • The nature of fate and human agency
  • The importance of xenia (guest-host relationships)
  • The concept of kleos (glory) and timē (honor)

The Iliad

The Iliad Summary

The Iliad takes place during the tenth and final year of the Trojan War. The conflict began when Paris, prince of Troy, abducted (or eloped with) Helen, wife of Menelaus, king of Sparta. Menelaus's brother, Agamemnon, gathered a massive Greek force to retrieve Helen and punish Troy. After nine years of siege and battles, the poem opens with the Greek army camped outside Troy's walls.


The epic begins not with the war's origins but with a conflict between Agamemnon and Achilles, the greatest of the Greek warriors. When Agamemnon is forced to surrender his war prize, the maiden Chryseis, he compensates himself by taking Briseis, Achilles' prize. This violation of honor causes Achilles to withdraw from battle, bringing disaster to the Greek forces.

Book 1: The Rage of Achilles

The opening establishes the poem's central theme: "the rage of Achilles." Apollo sends a plague upon the Greek camp because Agamemnon has refused to return Chryseis to her father, a priest of Apollo. When Calchas the seer reveals the cause, Agamemnon reluctantly returns the girl but demands Achilles' prize, Briseis, as compensation. Achilles, enraged at this dishonor, withdraws from battle and asks his divine mother, Thetis, to petition Zeus to aid the Trojans until Achilles' honor is restored.


Books 2-4: Mounting Tensions

Agamemnon tests his troops' resolve with a false suggestion to return home, which almost backfires. The armies prepare for battle, with a catalog of ships listing the Greek contingents (a passage of enormous cultural significance for later Greeks tracing their ancestry). A duel between Paris and Menelaus nearly ends the war, but Aphrodite rescues Paris. The truce is broken when the Trojan Pandarus wounds Menelaus, and full battle commences.


Books 5-7: Aristeia and Duels

These books feature Diomedes' aristeia (a hero's finest moments in battle), during which he wounds even the gods Aphrodite and Ares. Hector returns to Troy briefly, sharing touching moments with his wife Andromache and infant son Astyanax—a poignant contrast to the brutality of war. The first day of renewed fighting concludes with a duel between Hector and Ajax, ending in a respectful draw.


Books 8-10: Night Maneuvers

Zeus fulfills his promise to Thetis, turning the tide against the Greeks. Agamemnon considers retreat but is dissuaded. An embassy to Achilles' tent (Odysseus, Ajax, and Phoenix) fails to persuade him to return despite offers of rich gifts and the return of Briseis. Book 10 recounts a night mission by Odysseus and Diomedes, who kill the Trojan spy Dolon and raid the camp of Trojan allies.


Books 11-18: The Tide Turns

As the battle worsens for the Greeks, several leaders are wounded. Patroclus, Achilles' closest friend, begs to borrow Achilles' armor to inspire the Greeks. Achilles agrees but warns him only to drive the Trojans from the ships. Patroclus, caught up in battle frenzy, pursues the Trojans to their walls where Hector, with Apollo's help, kills him and takes Achilles' armor. When Achilles learns of Patroclus' death, his grief and rage transform his motivation—now seeking vengeance rather than honor's restoration.


Books 19-22: Achilles' Vengeance

Achilles reconciles with Agamemnon but refuses food or rest until he has avenged Patroclus. With new divine armor crafted by Hephaestus, including the elaborately decorated shield (an artistic microcosm of Greek life), Achilles returns to battle with unparalleled fury. He slaughters so many Trojans that the river Scamander rises against him. Finally, Achilles confronts Hector outside Troy's walls. Despite Hector's moment of panic and flight, they duel, and Achilles kills him. In his rage, Achilles drags Hector's body behind his chariot, refusing proper burial rites.


Books 23-24: Funeral Games and Ransom

Elaborate funeral games honor Patroclus, showcasing various athletic competitions central to Greek culture. In the poem's moving conclusion, Priam, guided by Hermes, comes to Achilles' tent to ransom his son's body. Achilles, touched by Priam's appeal to remember his own father, relents. The epic ends with Hector's funeral, a momentary pause in a war that both audience and characters know will end with Troy's destruction.


The Illiad Themes

At its core, Homer's Iliad tells the story of Achilles' destructive rage and its far-reaching consequences. This anger clouds his judgment, causing immense suffering not only for himself—most painfully in the loss of his beloved Patroclus—but also for his fellow Greeks who struggle without their greatest warrior. The ancient Greek heroes live by a strict code centered on timē (honor) and the pursuit of kleos (glory) through battlefield excellence. This pursuit of lasting fame drives much of the action, as warriors seek to build legacies that will outlive their mortal bodies. Yet through Achilles' emotional journey, the poem subtly questions whether honor alone provides life's true meaning. Throughout the narrative, we see characters grappling with predetermined fates while still exercising their free will. Achilles consciously chooses his short but glorious life over longevity without renown. Similarly, Hector faces Achilles in combat despite knowing Troy's ultimate destruction is inevitable. This unresolved tension between divine determination and human choice creates the story's dramatic power. The gods themselves are constant presences, intervening in human affairs according to their own interests and allegiances. Yet interestingly, these divine beings often display pettiness while the humans demonstrate remarkable nobility despite their limitations. The divine dimension adds complexity rather than clarity to the moral universe. Perhaps most profound is the Iliad's meditation on mortality itself. In the final books, we witness a transformative moment between enemies—Achilles and King Priam—who recognize their common humanity through shared grief and loss. This powerful scene suggests that how we face our inevitable mortality, and how we connect with others in our brief time, ultimately defines us more than any battlefield achievement.

The Odyssey

The Odyssey Summary

The Odyssey picks up after the Trojan War's conclusion, focusing on Odysseus's troubled ten-year journey home to Ithaca, where his wife Penelope and son Telemachus contend with arrogant suitors consuming Odysseus's household resources. Unlike the Iliad's straightforward chronology, The Odyssey employs a complex narrative structure with flashbacks, parallel storylines, and embedded tales.


Books 1-4: The Telemachy

The epic opens with the gods discussing Odysseus's fate. Athena, disguised as Mentes, advises Telemachus to seek news of his father from Nestor of Pylos and Menelaus of Sparta. In Pylos, Nestor recounts some veterans' returns but has no news of Odysseus. In Sparta, Menelaus and Helen share stories of Odysseus's cunning at Troy. Helen's ambiguous role is revisited—she seems simultaneously regretful of the war and still somewhat duplicitous. Meanwhile, the suitors plot to ambush and kill Telemachus upon his return.


Books 5-8: Odysseus Leaves Calypso

Odysseus is revealed on Calypso's island, where he has spent seven years. Though offered immortality, he yearns for home. At Zeus's command, Calypso releases him. He builds a raft but is shipwrecked by Poseidon (who harbors anger over Odysseus blinding his son Polyphemus). The sea-nymph Ino saves him, and he washes ashore in Phaeacia, where Princess Nausicaa discovers him. At the court of King Alcinous, Odysseus (initially anonymous) hears a bard sing of the Trojan War. When he weeps, he's pressed to reveal his identity, which begins his first-person narrative of wanderings.


Books 9-12: Odysseus's Wanderings

In extended flashback, Odysseus recounts his adventures since leaving Troy:


  • The Cicones: His men raid and linger too long, resulting in casualties
  • The Lotus-Eaters: Some men eat the lotus and lose desire to return home
  • The Cyclops Polyphemus: Odysseus blinds the Cyclops after being trapped in his cave, but unwisely reveals his name, earning Poseidon's enmity
  • Aeolus: The wind-god gives Odysseus a bag of winds, but his curious crew opens it near Ithaca, blowing them off course
  • The Laestrygonians: These giant cannibals destroy all but Odysseus's ship
  • Circe: The enchantress turns his men to swine but becomes Odysseus's lover and advisor, directing him to the Underworld
  • The Underworld: Odysseus speaks with dead heroes and his mother, and receives prophecy from Tiresias
  • The Sirens: Odysseus hears their song while tied to the mast
  • Scylla and Charybdis: Six men are lost to the six-headed monster Scylla
  • Thrinacia: Despite warnings, Odysseus's starving crew slaughters the sacred cattle of Helios; Zeus destroys their ship with a thunderbolt, leaving only Odysseus alive

Books 13-16: Return to Ithaca

The Phaeacians deliver Odysseus to Ithaca, where Athena disguises him as a beggar and directs him to the hut of his loyal swineherd, Eumaeus. Telemachus, warned by Athena of the suitors' ambush, returns safely and meets Odysseus, who reveals himself to his son. They plan revenge against the 108 suitors.


Books 17-20: The Beggar in the Palace

Still disguised, Odysseus enters his palace as a beggar. He endures abuse from the suitors and his disloyal servants while observing the situation. Recognition scenes occur with his aged dog Argos (who dies after seeing his master) and his old nurse Eurycleia (who identifies him by a childhood scar while washing his feet). Penelope, though suspicious, doesn't recognize him but proposes a contest using Odysseus's bow.


Books 21-24: Vengeance and Reconciliation

Penelope announces she will marry whoever can string Odysseus's bow and shoot an arrow through twelve axe heads. The suitors fail, but the disguised Odysseus succeeds. He then reveals himself, and with Telemachus, Eumaeus, and another loyal herdsman, slaughters all the suitors. He reunites with Penelope, who tests his identity by claiming to have moved their immovable bed. When Odysseus correctly describes how he built the bed around a living olive tree, she accepts him. After visiting his aged father Laertes, Odysseus faces the suitors' angry relatives, but Athena intervenes to establish peace.


The Illiad Themes

The Odyssey is a profound exploration of homecoming. The Greek concept of nostos permeates every aspect of Odysseus's ten-year voyage back to Ithaca after the Trojan War. This homecoming operates on multiple levels—not just geographical but psychological and social. Odysseus must not only reach his homeland physically but also reclaim his true identity after years spent in disguise and wandering. He must reestablish himself as king, husband to Penelope, and father to Telemachus. This multilayered struggle would have deeply resonated with ancient Greeks who knew firsthand the challenges of reintegration after prolonged absence due to war. Unlike the brute strength valorized in The Iliad through Achilles, Odysseus triumphs through his mētis—his cunning intelligence and adaptability. His epithet polytropos ("many-turning" or "of many devices") captures this essential quality. Throughout his journey, Odysseus relies on wit, deception, and resourcefulness rather than physical might alone. While the narrative celebrates these intellectual virtues, it also acknowledges their moral complexities, particularly when Odysseus's pride and curiosity place his men in danger. The epic weaves a rich tapestry of loyalty and temptation through its characters. Penelope's unwavering faithfulness during twenty years of separation stands as the poem's moral center, complemented by Telemachus's devoted search for his father and Eumaeus's steadfast service. These examples contrast sharply with the disloyal servants who align with the suitors and Odysseus's own men who succumb to various temptations. Though Odysseus himself has physical relationships with Circe and Calypso, he remains psychologically committed to returning home, refusing immortality for the chance to see Ithaca once more. Sacred hospitality (xenia) emerges as another crucial theme, reflecting its central importance in Greek culture. The poem presents both exemplary hosts like the Phaeacians and egregious violations by the Cyclops Polyphemus and the suitors who plague Odysseus's household. These contrasts suggest that civilization itself depends on properly honoring the guest-host relationship. The suitors' eventual slaughter is partly justified by their flagrant abuse of these sacred customs. The interplay between divine influence and human responsibility creates a nuanced moral landscape. While gods—particularly Athena as Odysseus's patron and Poseidon as his nemesis—shape events throughout, humans remain accountable for their choices. Odysseus's companions perish through their own foolishness, and the suitors face justice for their transgressions. Yet divine assistance proves essential to Odysseus's ultimate success, portraying human achievement as a delicate collaboration between mortal effort and divine favor. The narrative culminates in a series of recognition scenes where Odysseus's true identity is gradually revealed—to his faithful dog Argos, his old nurse Eurycleia, and finally to the cautious Penelope. These moments explore how identity is confirmed after prolonged absence and highlight the tension between appearance and reality. The mutual testing between Odysseus and Penelope—culminating in her clever bed trick—demonstrates their shared intelligence and appropriate wariness, qualities that make them truly matched partners.
