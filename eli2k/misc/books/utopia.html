Utopia ~ 1516

Last updated: May 16, 2025

Introduction and Historical Context

"Utopia" was written by Sir Thomas More (1478-1535), an English lawyer, social philosopher, author, statesman, and Renaissance humanist who served as Lord High Chancellor of England from 1529 to 1532 under King Henry VIII. Published in Latin in 1516, "Utopia" emerged during a period of significant political, religious, and intellectual change in Europe—the Renaissance and early Reformation era.

More wrote this work during the reign of Henry VIII, before their relationship deteriorated over the king's break with the Catholic Church. At the time, Europe was experiencing the aftermath of Columbus's voyages, expanding global exploration, and the beginnings of colonialism—all of which influenced More's depiction of a fictional traveler discovering an unknown island society.

The early 16th century was also marked by political instability in England and Europe, intense religious debates, and growing criticism of social inequality and corruption. More, as a devout Catholic and political insider, was uniquely positioned to observe the flaws in European society while maintaining his humanist ideals of education and reason.

Structure and Literary Form

"Utopia" is divided into two books, with the first serving as a frame narrative that features a fictionalized version of More himself engaged in conversation with the character Raphael Hythloday, a world traveler who has discovered the island of Utopia. This structure allows More to present radical ideas while maintaining plausible deniability about his own opinions—a necessary precaution given the politically sensitive nature of some of his critiques.

Book I contains a scathing criticism of 16th-century European society, particularly England, addressing issues such as the harsh treatment of thieves, the enclosure movement that displaced farmers, economic inequality, and the moral failings of European politics. Hythloday argues with More and his friend Peter Giles about whether philosophers should serve kings, suggesting that royal courts are too corrupt for genuine reform.

Book II consists of Hythloday's detailed description of Utopian society, including its geography, cities, government, economy, social customs, religion, and approach to war. The name "Utopia" itself is a play on Greek words, potentially meaning both "good place" (eu-topos) and "no place" (ou-topos)—a deliberate ambiguity that suggests More's awareness that his ideal society might be unattainable.

As a literary form, "Utopia" essentially created the genre of utopian fiction, which would later influence works ranging from Francis Bacon's "New Atlantis" to modern science fiction. It combines elements of travelogue, philosophical dialogue, social satire, and political theory.

Book I: Critique of European Society

The first book opens with Thomas More, narrating in first person, recounting how he meets his friend Peter Giles in Antwerp during a diplomatic mission. Giles introduces him to Raphael Hythloday, a Portuguese traveler who sailed with Amerigo Vespucci and claims to have discovered many unknown lands, including Utopia.

Their conversation quickly turns to politics, with Hythloday explaining why he refuses to serve as a royal advisor despite his knowledge. He argues that European monarchs are more interested in war and expansion than in good governance, and that their courts are filled with flatterers who resent outside wisdom.

Hythloday provides specific criticisms of English society, particularly focusing on:

  • The harsh punishment of thieves by hanging, which fails to address the root causes of theft
  • The enclosure movement, where common lands are privatized for sheep farming, displacing peasants who then have no choice but to steal for survival
  • The creation of idle nobles and their servants, who contribute nothing productive to society
  • The maintenance of standing armies that drain resources during peace and cause havoc during war

More (the character) challenges Hythloday, suggesting that he could do more good by working within the system, but Hythloday insists that genuine philosophical truth cannot survive in royal courts. This debate about the philosopher's role in politics reflects More's own dilemma as a humanist intellectual who also served as a royal councilor.

The first book serves as both a searing indictment of contemporary European society and a rhetorical setup for the alternative society described in Book II. By establishing what is wrong with Europe, More creates a context for understanding the radical differences of Utopian society.

Book II: The Utopian Society

The second book provides Hythloday's comprehensive description of Utopia—a crescent-shaped island with 54 identical cities. Each aspect of Utopian society represents either a direct solution to European problems identified in Book I or a radical alternative to European customs.

Physical layout and government:

  • Cities are perfectly planned, identical, and located no more than a day's walk from each other
  • The capital, Amaurot, sits at the center of the island
  • Each city is governed by elected officials and representatives
  • Thirty households elect a Syphogrant (magistrate), and ten Syphogrants are supervised by a Tranibor
  • The Prince (ruler) is elected for life by these representatives, but can be removed for tyranny

Economy and labor:

  • Private property is abolished—all goods belong to the community
  • Houses are rotated among families every ten years to prevent attachment to property
  • Everyone works in agriculture for two years and then practices a trade
  • The six-hour workday provides sufficient production while allowing time for learning
  • All citizens wear the same simple clothing, eliminating status competition
  • Gold and silver are deliberately devalued by using them for chamber pots and chains for slaves

Social customs:

  • Meals are taken communally in large halls
  • Healthcare is public and free to all citizens
  • Euthanasia is permitted for those with painful, incurable conditions (with their consent)
  • Marriage includes premarital "viewing" of potential partners naked to avoid later disappointment
  • Divorce is permitted in cases of adultery or extreme incompatibility
  • Travel between cities requires permission and a specific purpose

Education and knowledge:

  • Universal education in the native language (rather than Latin) for all citizens
  • Pre-dawn lectures that citizens attend voluntarily before work
  • Leisure time devoted to learning and intellectual pursuits
  • Scientific inquiry and practical knowledge are highly valued

Religion and ethics:

  • Religious tolerance with some broad requirements (belief in divine providence and soul immortality)
  • Multiple religions coexist peacefully, with shared temples and minimal ritual
  • Pleasure is accepted as a natural good, though "base pleasures" are discouraged
  • Moral philosophy centers on the pursuit of happiness through virtue and reason

The Utopians' approach to war and foreign relations reveals further ethical considerations. They avoid war when possible but will fight to defend themselves or allies. They prefer to hire mercenaries (particularly the Zapoletes) rather than risk their own citizens, and they use their gold reserves primarily to pay these fighters or bribe enemies. When they must fight, they target enemy leaders rather than common soldiers to minimize bloodshed.

Problematic Aspects of Utopia

While presenting an apparently ideal society, More includes several troubling elements that contemporary readers find particularly problematic:

  • Slavery: Utopia maintains a slave class consisting of war captives, criminals from other nations, and Utopians convicted of serious crimes. These slaves perform the most unpleasant work, wear distinctive badges, and face harsh punishment for rebellion.
  • Colonialism: When Utopia becomes overpopulated, they establish colonies on "uninhabited" mainland territories. If the native inhabitants resist, the Utopians wage war to take the land, justifying this as proper use of "idle" resources.
  • Limited freedom: Citizens require permission to travel between cities, are assigned to specific occupations, and face public shaming or slavery for breaking rules. There is little room for individuality or privacy.
  • Patriarchal family structure: Despite some progressive elements, Utopian households are led by the oldest male, wives serve husbands, and women confess their sins to their husbands monthly.

These contradictions raise questions about whether More genuinely endorsed all aspects of his fictional society or was subtly critiquing the very concept of engineered social perfection. The presence of these flaws suggests that "Utopia" might be as much a warning about the dangers of social engineering as it is a blueprint for reform.

Literary Devices and Ambiguity

More employs various literary techniques that create deliberate ambiguity about his own position on the ideas presented:

The most significant source of ambiguity is More's use of fictional personas, including himself as a character. By having Hythloday present radical ideas while "More" the character sometimes disagrees, the real More creates plausible deniability about his own views. The name "Raphael Hythloday" itself combines the archangel Raphael (guide and healer) with a Greek term meaning "speaker of nonsense," suggesting both wisdom and unreliability.

Other wordplay includes:

  • Utopia (no place/good place)
  • The Anyder River (no water)
  • Amaurot (phantom city)
  • Ademus (no people)
  • The Zapoletes (busy sellers—of themselves as mercenaries)

This pervasive wordplay suggests that More was engaged in an elaborate intellectual game, inviting readers to question rather than simply accept the society he describes. The fact that the book concludes with the character More stating he cannot agree with everything Hythloday has said further complicates interpretation.

The framing also serves a practical purpose: by presenting radical ideas as a traveler's account of a distant land, More could explore controversial concepts while maintaining some protection from political consequences—though this strategy ultimately failed when he later opposed Henry VIII's religious policies.

Philosophical Foundations and Influences

"Utopia" reflects a sophisticated synthesis of various philosophical traditions:

  • Platonic thought: The concept of philosopher-rulers, the emphasis on education, and the communal living arrangements echo Plato's "Republic."
  • Christian humanism: More's emphasis on education, virtue, and the application of reason to social problems reflects the humanist movement of which he was a part.
  • Catholic social teaching: Despite some seemingly radical elements, many Utopian practices align with Catholic ideals of community, charity, and care for the disadvantaged.
  • Epicureanism: The Utopian view that pleasure (properly understood) is the goal of life shows influence from Epicurean philosophy, which More reinterprets through a Christian lens.
  • Monastic tradition: The communal living, shared meals, and regulated lifestyle of Utopia parallel certain aspects of monastic communities.

More likely drew inspiration from real-world examples as well, including monastic communities, the Inca Empire (reports of which were just reaching Europe), and various indigenous societies encountered during European exploration. The work represents an attempt to imagine how Christian and classical ideals might be implemented in a society untainted by European corruption.

The philosophical ambiguity of the text makes it difficult to determine exactly which ideas More personally endorsed. Was he advocating for communal property or merely using it as a thought experiment? Did he genuinely believe in religious tolerance or was he illustrating what might happen without proper Church authority? This complexity has allowed "Utopia" to be claimed by various political traditions from socialism to Catholic conservatism.

Cultural Impact and Legacy

"Utopia" has had an enormous and enduring impact on Western culture and thought:

The term "utopia" itself has entered common language to describe any idealized society or perfect system. More's work established utopian literature as a distinct genre that continues to this day, influencing:

  • Later Renaissance utopias like Bacon's "New Atlantis" and Campanella's "City of the Sun"
  • Enlightenment political theory, including early socialist thought
  • Modern science fiction, particularly social science fiction that explores alternative societies
  • Dystopian literature, which often responds to or critiques utopian ideals
  • Practical communal experiments, from 19th-century utopian communities to aspects of 20th-century planned economies

More's work also influenced political thought across the spectrum. Some see in Utopia early seeds of socialist and communist thought with its abolition of private property. Others focus on its emphasis on family, virtue, and religious values as conservative elements. Some libertarian critics view it as an early warning about the dangers of collectivism and social engineering.

The enduring relevance of "Utopia" lies in its exploration of perennial questions: Can society be rationally designed? What is the proper balance between individual freedom and collective welfare? How should wealth be distributed? What role should religion play in public life? Five centuries after its publication, we are still debating these fundamental issues.

More's Personal Fate and Irony

There is profound irony in More's personal story following the publication of "Utopia." The man who imagined a society with religious tolerance would later become known for his persecution of Protestant reformers as Lord Chancellor. The author who questioned the wisdom of philosophers serving kings would ultimately lose his life for refusing to accommodate Henry VIII's break with the Catholic Church.

After refusing to acknowledge Henry VIII as Supreme Head of the Church of England and to validate the king's marriage to Anne Boleyn, More was convicted of treason and beheaded in 1535. His final words reportedly included the declaration that he died "the king's good servant, but God's first"—a statement that captures the fundamental tension explored in "Utopia" between ideal principles and political reality.

More was canonized as a Catholic saint in 1935, recognized for his unwavering commitment to his conscience. This transformation—from the humanist author of a work questioning established order to a martyr defending Church authority—reflects the complex political and religious dynamics of the Tudor period and adds another layer of interpretive complexity to "Utopia."

Modern readers might find it difficult to reconcile the seemingly progressive ideas in "Utopia" with More's conservative religious stance later in life. However, this apparent contradiction may help us understand "Utopia" not as a straightforward political manifesto but as a more nuanced exploration of the limitations of both radical reform and established tradition.

Critical Interpretations Over Time

Interpretations of "Utopia" have evolved significantly over five centuries, reflecting changing political, religious, and social contexts:

  • 16th-17th centuries: Early readers often focused on the work as a humanist critique of specific social problems rather than as a blueprint for comprehensive reform. Some emphasized its satirical elements, while Catholic readers tended to see it as compatible with Church teaching despite its fictional religious tolerance.
  • Enlightenment era: As rationalism gained ground, "Utopia" was increasingly read as an early example of rational social design. Its communal ownership system attracted particular attention from early socialist thinkers.
  • 19th century: Marxist interpreters claimed More as a proto-socialist critic of early capitalism, particularly focusing on his critique of enclosure and private property. More conservative Victorian readers emphasized the disciplined, orderly nature of Utopian society.
  • 20th century: After the rise and fall of totalitarian regimes, some critics like Karl Popper viewed "Utopia" more critically as a potentially dangerous example of "social engineering." The mid-century saw renewed interest in the work's literary complexity and ambiguity.
  • Contemporary period: Recent scholarship has emphasized postcolonial critiques of Utopia's imperialism, feminist analyses of its gender politics, and renewed attention to More's religious motivations. Digital humanities approaches have also revealed new patterns in More's language and sources.

These varying interpretations remind us that each generation finds in "Utopia" a mirror for its own preoccupations. The work's enduring relevance stems partly from this interpretive flexibility—its ability to stimulate thought about social organization without providing simple answers.

Personal Reflection: Why Utopia Still Matters

When I first encountered "Utopia" as a student, I was struck by how many of More's 500-year-old observations still ring true today. His critique of economic inequality, his questioning of harsh criminal punishment, and his concern about the moral corruption of politics all feel remarkably current.

What makes "Utopia" continually relevant is not its specific proposals, many of which feel outdated or problematic to modern sensibilities. Rather, it's the fundamental questions the work poses: Is radical social change possible? Can we design better systems, or are we constrained by human nature? How do we balance individual freedom with collective welfare?

When I look at contemporary debates about universal basic income, criminal justice reform, work-life balance, and the proper scope of government, I see echoes of the same tensions More was exploring. His genius was not in providing definitive answers but in creating a framework for thinking about these issues that transcends his specific historical moment.

Perhaps the most valuable aspect of "Utopia" for today's readers is its demonstration that imagining alternatives is itself a powerful political act. Even if we reject aspects of More's fictional society, the very process of thinking systematically about how society might be otherwise organized helps us question arrangements we take for granted. In an era where "there is no alternative" has become a political mantra, More's willingness to imagine a completely different social order remains radical and necessary.

If you've never read "Utopia," I encourage you to approach it not as a historical curiosity but as a still-living conversation about human society—one that you're invited to join with your own critical perspective. The questions it raises are as urgent now as they were in 1516, and while we may not share More's answers, we continue to benefit from his questions.
